New Zealand cuisine doesn't have the global profile of Italian or Japanese food. This is partially unfair. Here is my honest accounting of the best things I ate during twelve months in Aotearoa.

The meat pie

A fundamental part of daily NZ life, not a novelty item for tourists. They live in every petrol station, dairy, and bakery. The good ones have short crust pastry that holds its shape, a filling that stays moist without becoming liquid, and real gravy. The bad ones are also fine, honestly.

Best I found: a steak-and-mushroom pie from a bakery in Hokitika whose name I didn't write down and now deeply regret. I've thought about it specifically many times since.

The flat white

Whether New Zealand or Australia invented it is a live dispute, and both sides care too much. What isn't disputed is that New Zealand makes them exceptionally well. A proper flat white — 30ml espresso, 130ml velvety steamed milk, correct ratio — is simply the standard everywhere. You don't have to ask for good coffee. You just get it. This is remarkable.

Hāngī

Traditional Māori earth oven: food wrapped in leaves and cloth, lowered into a pit of hot stones, covered for hours. The flavour is smoky and deeply savoury in a way that no conventional cooking method replicates. Best eaten at Te Pā Tū in Rotorua, or — if you're lucky — at someone's home.

Seafood

Crayfish tails at Kaikōura, grilled and eaten standing up at the pier. Pāua (abalone) fritters, which are chewy and oceanic and very good. Bluff oysters in winter — available only May to August, from the southernmost accessible point of the South Island, tasting of deep cold seawater in the most direct and pleasing way imaginable.

Bluff oysters taste exactly like the sea they came from. This is not a metaphor. It is the literal flavour. Eat them in their season.

Hokey Pokey ice cream

Vanilla ice cream with pieces of honeycomb toffee. The toffee shatters when you bite it. The vanilla is properly flavoured, not the synthetic kind. This is the standard against which all ice cream should be measured. I ate it more times than I tracked and feel no embarrassment about this.

Also worth having: L&P (a New Zealand lemon soft drink that tastes better than it sounds), kumara chips, Anzac biscuits, Tim Tams (yes they're Australian; they're everywhere here).
