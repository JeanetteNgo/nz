Most working holiday makers land in Auckland and immediately plan to leave. Get a SIM card, pick up a campervan, head south. This is understandable and also a mistake.

Auckland is not New Zealand's most dramatic city. Wellington is cooler. Christchurch is more interesting at the moment, mid-reinvention. But Auckland has the harbour, and the harbour changes everything.

The Waitematā Harbour from the waterfront at dusk, with the ferries crossing and the Sky Tower lit up and the North Shore hills going purple across the water — it's one of the best urban views I've seen anywhere.

Waiheke Island

Take the ferry. It takes thirty-five minutes and costs less than you expect. Waiheke has olive groves, vineyards, beaches, and a pace that is noticeably different from the mainland even though the mainland is thirty-five minutes away. I went for a day and stayed three.

Mudbrick Vineyard for lunch, if you can afford it. If you can't, the wines by the glass at the cellar door are reasonably priced and the views are free. The walking track between Oneroa and Palm Beach takes about two hours and gives you both coasts of the island.

Devonport

Another ferry, this one only twelve minutes. Devonport is a heritage suburb on the North Shore with Victorian villas, good bookshops, and two volcanic summits you can climb. North Head has old military tunnels and 360-degree views. Mount Victoria is smaller and has a playground at the top which felt appropriate.

Auckland from the water is a different city from Auckland on the motorway. Try to see it from the water as much as possible.

Practical notes

  • The AT HOP card works on ferries, buses and trains — get one immediately
  • The city is spread out; don't try to walk everywhere
  • Sky Tower views are expensive; the view from Mount Eden is free
  • Ponsonby Road for eating; Karangahape Road for everything else

Minimum stay: 3 days to do it properly · Day trip: Waiheke Island, always
