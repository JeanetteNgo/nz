Rotorua smells of sulphur and doesn't apologise for it. You stop noticing after an hour. The locals have never noticed it at all.

The city sits on one of the most geothermally active zones on Earth, and the earth makes itself known constantly: steam rising from pavements, geysers in the park behind the museum, bubbling mud pools in the suburbs. It sounds dramatic. It is, in a low-key way that makes the whole place feel like it's sitting on something barely contained.

Te Puia

The most visited geothermal site in the region, and for good reason. Pōhutu geyser — the largest active geyser in the Southern Hemisphere — erupts up to twenty times a day, sending water twenty metres into the air. There's no reliable schedule. You wait, and it goes, and it's worth it every time.

Te Puia is also home to the New Zealand Māori Arts and Crafts Institute, where you can watch carving and weaving being practised and taught. This part of the visit is quieter than the geyser crowds and more interesting.

The hāngī

Rotorua is the best place in New Zealand to eat a proper hāngī — the traditional Māori earth oven. Food is wrapped in cloth, placed in baskets, and lowered into a pit heated by geothermal steam. It cooks for hours. The result is smoky, deeply savoury, and unlike anything made by conventional heat.

I've eaten hāngī twice in my life — once at a Rotorua restaurant, once at a family gathering I was lucky to be invited to. The second was better. But the first was still excellent.

Wai-O-Tapu

Half an hour south of Rotorua, this is the most visually extraordinary geothermal site I visited. The Champagne Pool — a 65-metre wide crater lake of acid-hot water the colour of liquid copper and green — looks like a special effect. The Lady Knox Geyser erupts at 10:15am daily, which slightly diminishes the wonder, but the walk around the rest of the site is genuinely strange and beautiful.

Distance from Auckland: 3 hours south · Budget: Te Puia entry ~NZ$60; Wai-O-Tapu ~NZ$38
