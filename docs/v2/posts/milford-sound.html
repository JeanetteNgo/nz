Everyone says go on a clear day. They're wrong.

I went on a day of heavy rain, arriving at the wharf through a valley so full of low cloud you couldn't see the peaks. The rain had been continuous for eighteen hours. Every travel forum I'd consulted suggested this was a waste of time. It was not.

When it rains at Milford Sound, the waterfalls multiply. Every cliff face becomes a cascade. The permanent waterfalls — Stirling, Lady Bowen, Fairy — become enormous. Temporary waterfalls appear by the hundreds, pouring off ledges that are usually dry, running in white threads down the dark rock. The fiord fills with a low mist that the boat moves through, and the scale of the cliffs — 1,200 metres from water to summit — becomes even harder to process.

Milford Sound in the rain is not a compromise. It's a different and possibly better version of the place.

The drive in

The road from Te Anau through the Homer Tunnel is itself remarkable. The tunnel is one-lane, unlined rock, and drops 945 metres over about a kilometre. Coming out the other side, the valley opens and the scale of Fiordland becomes suddenly clear. Pull over. Take your time.

The Chasm, a short walk from the road about fifteen minutes from Milford, is worth the stop. A river has cut impossible shapes through the rock. Free, ten-minute walk, genuinely astonishing.

The boat cruise

Opt for a smaller boat if you can. The big tourist vessels are fine, but a smaller catamaran gets closer to the waterfalls and is quieter. Most cruises take two hours. The overnight cruise — sleeping on the fiord — is, by all accounts, extraordinary, though I didn't do it.

  • Sandflies are aggressive at the wharf and in the carpark — bring repellent
  • Book accommodation in Te Anau (not at the Sound) for better options and prices
  • Road access can close briefly after heavy snow — check conditions

Distance from Te Anau: 119 km, 2 hours · Boat cruise: 2 hours, book ahead in summer
