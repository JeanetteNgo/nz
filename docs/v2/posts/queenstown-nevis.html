Queenstown knows what it is. It has leaned entirely into being beautiful and thrilling, and done so with such conviction that it's impossible to hold it against the place.

Lake Wakatipu is impossibly blue. The Remarkables range defines the skyline to the southeast, jagged and snow-dusted, living up to their name with deliberate showmanship. The town itself is small enough to walk everywhere and busy enough that you never feel lonely in it.

The Nevis

I did the Nevis bungy. This was not a casual decision. I made it on a Tuesday when I was feeling reckless, then had to execute it on Thursday when I was not. The Nevis is 134 metres — the highest bungy jump in New Zealand — suspended from a cable car over a remote canyon. You get there by 4WD.

The platform is enclosed, glass-floored, and very high. You stand in a harness while someone checks your ankles and cheerfully tells you to enjoy the view. You step off backwards because the forward view apparently causes second thoughts.

You do not think about anything in the three seconds before the cord catches. Your brain, sensibly, refuses to participate.

I lay on the recovery mat for four minutes afterwards feeling genuinely excellent about life in a way that I have not been able to replicate since. The video they sell you afterwards is extremely unflattering. I bought it anyway.

The slower side

Queenstown rewards slowing down. The gondola up to Bob's Peak gives views of the lake and town that justify the price entirely. Sit on the terrace with a coffee and stay longer than you planned to.

Arrowtown, twenty minutes out, is a Victorian gold-rush village with excellent pie shops and autumn trees that go properly gold. It's worth half a day at least. The Lakes District Museum there is small and genuinely good.

Getting there

Queenstown has its own airport. If you're driving from Christchurch, the road via the Lindis Pass takes about five hours and is beautiful. The Crown Range road from Wānaka is shorter and even more dramatic, but closes in snow.
