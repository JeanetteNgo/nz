The 2011 earthquake killed 185 people and destroyed the centre of a city. Fourteen years later, Christchurch is still rebuilding — and the rebuilding is, genuinely, interesting.

The central city is a patchwork: glass-and-steel new buildings next to vacant lots next to heritage façades held up by steel frames, waiting for restoration. The Transitional Cathedral — a church built from cardboard tubes by the Japanese architect Shigeru Ban — has become a permanent fixture because people liked it too much to take it down. This feels right.

The Botanic Gardens

Free. Open every day. On a clear autumn afternoon, the best place in New Zealand to walk slowly and not think about anything in particular. The rose garden peaks in November and December. The café by the fernery does good coffee.

Art and galleries

The Christchurch Art Gallery re-opened in 2015 after earthquake repairs and is excellent — particularly the New Zealand contemporary collection. Free entry for the permanent collection. The street art around the city is also worth a deliberate walk; some of it is very good.

There's a particular kind of resilience that doesn't announce itself. Christchurch has it. The city is getting on with things, and the getting-on is visible in its streets.

Day trips

Akaroa, ninety minutes south-east, is a former French settlement on the rim of an extinct volcano. The harbour is calm, the food is good, and the heritage buildings are well-preserved. Go mid-week to avoid the tour buses.

Hanmer Springs, ninety minutes north, has thermal pools in an alpine setting. The town itself is touristy but the pools are genuinely relaxing, especially in winter when it's cold enough to want them.
